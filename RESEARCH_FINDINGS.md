## Patient Pain Points

### Theme 1: No Family Doctor / Primary Care Desert

**The Scale:**
- 50% of Canadians either lack a family doctor or struggle to see the one they have (Angus Reid, 2025/2026)
- 6.5 million Canadians have no regular family doctor or nurse practitioner
- 45% of those actively searching have been looking for over a year (up from 31% in 2015)
- 25% have given up entirely on finding a GP

**Pain Point 1: Doctor retires, patient orphaned**
Patients whose family doctor retires are dumped onto provincial waitlists with no timeline. Saskatchewan (63% difficulty), Quebec (60%), and Atlantic Canada (52%) are hardest hit. The workaround: community Facebook groups and Reddit threads where neighbors share tips about who is accepting patients. One Toronto Life article documented a person who literally found a family doctor through a stranger on Reddit because "a stranger on Reddit was more effective at getting me proper care than Ontario's referral system."

**Pain Point 2: Walk-in clinics as the only lifeline**
Without a family doctor, patients rely on walk-in clinics where they see a different doctor every time, repeat their history from scratch, and are limited to "one issue per visit." A patient described arriving in frozen weather, standing outside with no shelter, being 32nd in queue, and waiting 2+ hours.

**Emotional reality:** Helplessness, abandonment, anger at a system that claims to be "universal" but leaves millions without a primary care relationship.

**What they wish existed:** A way to actually find a doctor accepting patients, without relying on word-of-mouth luck.

Sources:
- Angus Reid Institute - Health Care Access 2026
- Toronto Life - Reddit Referral Story
- CBC - Meet Canadians Without a Family Doctor

### Theme 2: Catastrophic ER Wait Times

**The Scale:**
- ~500,000 Canadians left ERs before being seen by a doctor in 2024
- Ontario patients admitted to hospital spent an average of 20.3 hours in the ER before getting a bed
- Quebec ER stretcher time averaged 18 hours
- Winnipeg: 20+ hour waits reported

**Pain Point 3: Waiting with life-threatening symptoms**
A patient with heart pain: "We were shocked at how tiny the emergency waiting area was... I only waited two hours to be triaged and at least three hours before someone saw me." They feared death during the wait. Triage staff was described as dismissive and rude.

**Pain Point 4: Dying in the waiting room**
A New Brunswick man died after waiting 7 hours unattended in an ER (caught on video). An estimated 15,000 emergency room deaths per year are attributable to crowding.

**Pain Point 5: Discharged too soon, bounced back**
One patient described being septic with 16+ hour ER waits, then premature discharge, then readmission. "The doctor offered minimal help: 'I wish there was something more we could do.'"

**Emotional reality:** Terror, anger, disbelief. The feeling that the system will let you die in a waiting room chair.

**What they wish existed:** Actual triage that works. A way to know where to go that is not the ER. Real-time information about wait times and alternatives.

Sources:
- CBC - 500,000 Canadians Left ERs
- CBC - Hospital ERs at Breaking Point
- The Hub - Healthcare Horror Stories
- SecondStreet - Top 10 Worst Stories 2024

### Theme 3: Specialist Referral Black Hole

**The Scale:**
- Median wait from GP referral to treatment: 28.6 weeks (2025), down slightly from 30 weeks (2024)
- 56% of those needing specialist care in the past 6 months faced barriers
- Only 49% satisfied with specialist wait times; 31% dissatisfied
- 64% said their lives were affected by the wait

**Pain Point 6: Referrals that vanish**
One patient spent months thinking she was on a specialist waitlist, only to discover the referral was never received or was rejected — and nobody informed her. "Hundreds of Calgary urology reports lost due to fax machine malfunctions" was an actual 2024 headline.

**Pain Point 7: Years-long specialist waits with deteriorating health**
A Saskatchewan woman faced a year-long wait just to see a specialist for a severe stomach condition that prevented her from eating. Kim, from The Hub: "Because of this lack of timeliness, I will never see properly out of the eye" — delayed retinal surgery led to permanent vision loss, measured at 20/2100.

**Pain Point 8: Nobody tracks or communicates status**
Patients have no visibility into where they stand in a queue. Even when specialists reject referrals, patients are often not informed, so they wait months for an appointment that was never booked. The burden falls on the patient to chase down their own referral.

**Emotional reality:** Anxiety that turns to despair. The feeling of being invisible to the system. Health deteriorating while waiting.

**What they wish existed:** A way to track referral status. Notifications when something goes wrong. A system that does not rely on fax machines.

Sources:
- Fraser Institute - Wait Times 2025
- Statistics Canada - Wait Times for Specialists 2024
- CIHI - Canadians Not Getting Appointments Quickly

### Theme 4: Mental Health Access Crisis

**The Scale:**
- Ontario psychiatrist wait: ~4 months average
- Quebec: ~8 months for a psychologist or psychiatrist
- Newfoundland: up to 3 years for a psychiatrist
- Psychologists and therapists are NOT covered by provincial health plans

**Pain Point 9: Can't afford private, can't access public**
Public psychiatry has month/year-long waits. Psychologists and therapists are only covered privately. If you cannot afford $150-250/session, you are stuck in the public queue. One in five Canadians does not have the family doctor needed to even make the referral.

**Pain Point 10: Crisis with nowhere to go**
People in acute mental health crisis end up in ERs where the wait is 12-20 hours, the environment is chaotic, and mental health is triaged below physical emergencies.

**Emotional reality:** Desperation. The bitter irony of needing help for depression/anxiety while the system's inaccessibility makes both worse.

**What they wish existed:** Affordable, timely access to mental health support. A clear pathway that does not start with "get a referral from a family doctor you don't have."

Sources:
- Resolvve - Psychiatrist Wait Times Canada
- CBC - Wait Times Mental Health Waterloo-Wellington
- CBC - 3-Year Wait for Psychiatrist Stephenville

### Theme 5: Chronic Pain and Being Dismissed

**Pain Point 11: "Not taken seriously"**
The r/ChronicPain subreddit (131,000 subscribers) has a pinned post titled "how to get doctors to take you seriously." The Canadian Pain Task Force found that "many people living with pain feel stigmatized, scrutinized, and in some cases dismissed by health care professionals."

**Pain Point 12: Gender and racial bias in pain treatment**
Women, people of colour, and Indigenous patients are disproportionately dismissed. Women of colour report facing the most discrimination. One woman had approximately 50 doctor appointments, ultrasounds, or hospital visits, with doctors repeatedly saying "she simply didn't have a high enough pain tolerance and that nothing was wrong medically."

**Pain Point 13: Opioid crackdown catching legitimate patients**
Patients describe being tapered off effective medications without alternatives: "I have been reduced twice already and can barely function." Others report spending $17,500+ annually out-of-pocket for treatments their doctors refuse to prescribe.

**Emotional reality:** Feeling criminalized for being in pain. Depression, suicidal ideation. Descriptions of pain as "torture" and "a prison."

**What they wish existed:** Doctors who listen and believe. Timely access to pain specialists (current average: 18 months). Multidisciplinary pain clinics that are actually accessible.

Sources:
- Healthy Debate - Chronic Pain Care
- Canada.ca - Canadian Pain Task Force Report
- CBC - Invisible Illnesses Women Not Believed
- Reader's Digest Canada - Women's Pain Undiagnosed

### Theme 6: System Navigation Is Impossible

**Pain Point 14: Nobody tells you how the system works**
Research confirms "patients are required to identify an adequate entry point to the health care system, to orientate themselves within a multitude of organizations, to maneuver through the system, and to find the right place for their own problems." People with low health literacy experience "disorientation, futile and stressful searches, uncertainty, and discontinuities in health care."

**Pain Point 15: Walk-in sends you to ER, ER sends you to GP, GP sends you to specialist, specialist never calls**
The "one issue per visit" problem is documented: a doctor allegedly refused to hear a woman's complaint about chest pains because they had already discussed another issue. A week later, she had a heart attack and died. Patients describe being bounced between care settings with no coordination.

**Pain Point 16: Test results fall into the void**
Patients discovered life-threatening blood test indicators were out of normal range, yet none had been discussed with them. "No news is good news" remains common practice despite being explicitly against guidelines. Patients are forced to self-research serious test results via Google.

**Emotional reality:** Confusion, exhaustion, feeling like you need a medical degree just to get basic care. The burden of being your own case manager.

**What they wish existed:** A single clear guide for "what do I do when X." Someone or something that tracks your care across providers. Automatic notifications about test results and referral status.

Sources:
- PMC - Navigation Health Literacy
- CBC - One Issue Per Visit Problem
- CBC - No News Not Good News Test Results

### Theme 7: Caregiver Burden

**Pain Point 17: Family members become unpaid case managers**
Kathleen's husband was in 7 hospitals in 10 months. Despite being an inpatient, he was never able to get an appointment with a urologist. She had to manage catheter dependency and recurring infections while navigating a bureaucratic maze across multiple institutions.

**Pain Point 18: Long-distance caregiving compounds everything**
Globe and Mail reported on a 48-year-old single mother driving three hours from Toronto to a hospital in London, Ontario to advocate for her father. Caregivers must take time off work, manage their own families, and become experts in a system designed for professionals.

**Emotional reality:** Exhaustion, guilt, rage at a system that puts the coordination burden on the most vulnerable families.

**What they wish existed:** Patient navigators/advocates. A single point of contact. Care coordination that works across institutions.

Sources:
- The Hub - Healthcare Horror Stories
- Globe and Mail - Long-Distance Caregiving

### Theme 8: Newcomer / Immigrant Barriers

**Pain Point 19: 3-month coverage gap with no safety net**
New immigrants face a 3-month waiting period before provincial health insurance kicks in. Many do not know about this, do not purchase private insurance due to lack of knowledge, and face worsening health conditions — especially dangerous for pregnant women.

**Pain Point 20: Language, culture, and system design exclude newcomers**
Immigrants report feeling "overwhelmed while interacting with the healthcare system due to a lack of knowledge and lack of support to help them navigate the new system." They are more likely to have poor therapeutic relationships with providers, cannot communicate health needs effectively, develop mistrust, and experience racism.

**Emotional reality:** Vulnerability, isolation, fear of a system that is supposed to be their new home's safety net.

**What they wish existed:** Multilingual navigation support. Clear onboarding to the healthcare system as part of immigration. Cultural competency in care delivery.

Sources:
- PMC - Barriers to Healthcare for New Immigrants
- Western University - Immigrant Barriers to Healthcare
- ICA Victoria - Newcomers Navigating Healthcare Report

## Provider & Clinician Pain Points

### Theme 1: Documentation Burden (The #1 Pain Point)

**Pain Point 1: Documentation consumes more time than patient care**
- For every 15 minutes a physician spends with a patient, they spend 9 minutes charting in the EHR
- Physicians spend 5.8 hours in the EHR for every 8 hours of scheduled patient time
- Primary care physicians spend 3+ hours per day on clinical documentation alone
- Physicians would need 27 hours per day to complete all recommended care AND administrative requirements
- 42% of physicians cite documentation/EHR burden as the primary root cause of their burnout
- 70% of doctors report work stress affecting quality of life

**Pain Point 2: "Pajama time" — after-hours EHR work destroying personal lives**
- 85% of healthcare professionals engage in pajama time
- Average: 8.2 hours of after-hours EHR work per week (equivalent to 53.3 full workdays per year)
- Most time-consuming tasks: charting (56%), responding to patient messages (40%), reviewing labs/imaging (33%)
- 35% have considered leaving the profession due to EHR-related burnout
- 1 in 2 professionals have canceled or delayed personal plans
- 21% say it has caused conflict with partners or family

**Pain Point 3: Nurses drowning in data entry**
- Nurses spend approximately 40% of their shift on documentation
- They document 600-800 data points per 12-hour shift — one data point every 1.11 minutes
- 35% of nurses spend 3+ hours per week on unproductive charting
- 30,000 hours annually per health system are lost to documentation instead of direct patient care

Sources:
- AMA - Doctors work fewer hours but EHR follows them home
- Physician Burnout & Documentation Burden 2026 Statistics
- CMA - Administrative burden facts
- AACN - Nursing Documentation Burden

---

### Theme 2: EMR/EHR Usability Failures

**Pain Point 4: EHR systems are non-intuitive and require excessive clicks**
- 48% of clinicians say EHRs slow tasks due to poor workflow fit (HIMSS 2023)
- Nearly 75% of those with burnout symptoms point to the EHR as the source
- Over 50% of Ontario clinicians rated tech-related burnout at 7+ on a 0-10 scale
- Data entry errors account for 27% of usability challenges

**Pain Point 5: Poor interoperability — systems don't talk to each other**
- Only 28% of physicians say sending/receiving patient data on a different EHR is effective
- 71% of hospitals have access to external clinical info, but only 42% of clinicians routinely use it because it's too hard to find
- Only 16% of hospitals can send care records to long-term care providers
- 61% of physicians feel overloaded with information that increases stress, while lacking the RIGHT information at the RIGHT time

**Pain Point 6: Unmanageable inboxes and redundant notifications**
- Top 3 burdensome tech tasks for Ontario clinicians: (1) managing reports sent through EMR, (2) managing communications related to patient care, (3) inputting patient data
- Constant accessibility interrupts personal time
- Important messages get buried under noise; critical results can be missed

Sources:
- 2024 CMA/Infoway Survey on Administrative Burdens
- OntarioMD - Clinician Tech-Related Burnout Survey
- athenahealth - Interoperability Challenges

### Theme 3: Alert Fatigue

**Pain Point 7: Clinical decision support alerts are overwhelmingly ignored**
- Override rates reach 90-96% of all CDS alerts
- Primary care physicians receive a median of 63 alerts per day; VA clinicians receive 100+ per day
- ICU monitors generated 2 million alerts in one month across 66 beds — 187 warnings per patient per day
- A Boston Globe investigation found 200+ deaths over 5 years linked to failure to respond to monitoring alarms
- A teenager received a 38-fold overdose of an antibiotic because physicians ignored alerts
- Alerts are "only modestly effective at best"

Sources:
- AHRQ PSNet - Alert Fatigue Primer
- PMC - EHR Usability Challenges Scoping Review

### Theme 4: Administrative / Bureaucratic Burden

**Pain Point 8: Prior authorization is a massive time sink**
- Practices complete 39 prior authorization requests per physician per week, consuming 13 hours of physician/staff time weekly
- 35% of physicians employ staff working exclusively on prior auth
- 27% of requests are often or always denied
- 87% of physicians say prior auth increases overall healthcare utilization (the opposite of its intent)

**Pain Point 9: Patient portal messages have exploded post-COVID**
- Physicians get 57% more portal messages than pre-pandemic (157% increase overall)
- 70% of medical groups reported further increases in 2024
- Physicians in the top quartile for messages have 6x greater odds of burnout

**Pain Point 10: Sick notes and unnecessary paperwork (Canada-specific)**
- Canadian physicians spend 18.5 million hours on unnecessary administrative tasks every year
- Equivalent to 55.6 million patient visits
- 38% of the 48 million hours spent on admin tasks is deemed unnecessary
- 60% of CMA survey respondents say admin burden is a direct contributor to burnout

Sources:
- AMA - Prior Authorization Survey 2024
- AMA - Patient Portal Inbox and Burnout
- CMA - 20 million hours of unnecessary paperwork

**Pain Point 11: Family doctors fleeing practice in Canada**

**Pain Point 12: Nurse practitioner overload as scope expands**
- NPs report being overwhelmed by constant pressure to open new case files with focus solely on patient numbers, not complexity
- Insufficient admin staff: one clerical worker supporting three NPs
- Large and complex caseloads with limited support

### Theme 6: Moral Injury, Mental Health & Compassion Fatigue

**Pain Point 13: Moral injury from systemic failures**
- Healthcare workers experience moral injury from knowing what patients need but being unable to provide it
- Burnout prevalence: 70%+ among nurses and doctors, 56% in dietitians, 43% in emergency staff

**Pain Point 14: Burnout rates remain crisis-level**
- 46% of Canadian physicians report high burnout (2025 survey), down from 53% in 2021 but still far above the 30% baseline in 2017
- Specialty-specific: Emergency Medicine 65%, Family Medicine 50%, Internal Medicine 49%
- Physician replacement cost: $500,000-$1 million per person
- Female physicians experience burnout 27% higher than males

**Pain Point 15: Healthcare workforce hemorrhaging talent**
- Canada's health workforce grew by only 2.7% (45,000 workers) in 2024, insufficient to meet demand
- Rural areas face disproportionate challenges
- Those who stay face ever-increasing workloads as colleagues leave

Sources:
- CMA - 2025 National Physician Health Survey
- Medscape - Half of Canadian Physicians Report High Burnout
- CIHI - State of the Health Workforce in Canada 2024

- Same data collected multiple times across different forms and platforms
- Increases risk of transcription errors, delays care

## Canadian Healthcare System Data

### Access / Front Door

- ~6.5 million Canadians lack a regular family doctor (2024 CMA estimates)
- Problem worst in rural/remote areas, Atlantic provinces, Northern Ontario/BC
- Emergency department waits: median time-to-physician 2-3 hours in urban EDs; time-to-admission often exceeded 8-12 hours
- Median wait for hip replacement ~6 months, knee replacement similar

**Existing infrastructure:**
- Ontario Health811: Nurse-staffed telephone/online triage
- BC HealthLink (811), Quebec Info-Sante (811), Telehealth Ontario, Alberta Health Link
- Don't rebuild 811. Build tools that integrate with or augment existing triage infrastructure.

### Care Closer to Patient

- Virtual care: roughly 15-25% of primary care visits still virtual (down from ~60% during COVID peak)
- Ontario's virtual care billing codes became permanent
- Federal bilateral health agreements: $46.2B over 10 years to provinces — home care and mental health as priority pillars
- Team-based primary care models: Ontario Health Teams (50+), BC Primary Care Networks, Alberta PCNs, Quebec GMFs

### Workforce

- Nursing shortage: tens of thousands of unfilled positions nationally
- Medical graduates increasingly choosing specialties over family med
- Federal focus on credential recognition for internationally educated health professionals
- AI-assisted documentation is the lowest-friction, highest-value entry point for AI in healthcare (30-50% time reduction in pilots)

### Operational Efficiency

- Canada spends ~$8,500-$9,000 CAD per capita on healthcare (~$330B+ annually)
- Commonwealth Fund consistently ranks Canada poorly on value-for-money
- Administrative overhead estimated at 15-30%
- Post-COVID surgical backlog: 500,000+ cases nationally (2022-2023 estimates)
- "Hallway medicine" persists — ALC patients occupy 15-20% of acute beds in some hospitals
- FHIR adoption increasing but uneven; many systems still on HL7v2

### Provincial Leadership

| Area | Leading Province(s) | Notes |
|------|---------------------|-------|
| Team-based primary care | Ontario (OHTs), BC (PCNs) | Most structured, most funded |
| Virtual care policy | Ontario, BC | Permanent billing codes |
| Surgical recovery | Ontario, BC | Dedicated programs and funding |
| Digital health infrastructure | Ontario (ConnectingOntario), Alberta (Netcare) | Most mature provincial clinical viewers |
| Home care investment | All (federal bilateral $) | Varies by province |
| AI pilots | Ontario, Quebec | Hospital-based pilots most visible |

### Where Money Is Flowing

1. Federal bilateral agreements: $46.2B over 10 years — family health, mental health, home care, workforce, digital health
2. Provincial surgical recovery funds (ON, BC, AB)
3. Digital health modernization via Infoway + provincial investments
4. Health workforce: credential recognition, training seats, retention bonuses

**Key insight:** Canada's healthcare system doesn't lack money — it lacks coordination, efficiency, and workforce capacity. Technology that multiplies existing resources beats technology that adds new ones.

## Healthcare Operations Pain Points

### 1. The Referral Black Hole

- **50% of specialist referrals are never completed** (IHI / NPSF)
- **65% of referrals never result in a scheduled appointment** (NCBI/PMC)
- Of 103,737 analyzed referral scheduling attempts, only **34.8% resulted in documented complete appointments**
- **56% of referrals are still sent by fax**; 45% handed on paper; 26% verbal only
- **69% of PCPs say they "always" send patient history** — yet **only 34% of specialists actually receive it**
- **Nearly 50% of malpractice claims** related to missed/delayed diagnoses involve failure to follow up on referrals
- Revenue leakage: **$800K-$900K lost per physician** from referrals that never convert
- Denver Health improved referral closure from **18% to 73.3%** with closed-loop tracking

Sources:
- Closed-Loop Referrals Guide - Planstreet
- 3 Patient Referral Pain Points - Phreesia
- Are Your Referrals Getting Lost - AAFP
- Closing the Referral Loop - PMC

---

### 2. The Fax Machine Problem (Canada-specific)

- **90%+ of Ontario doctors still use fax machines**
- Ontario providers send an estimated **152 million faxes per year**
- Fax persists because different EMRs cannot communicate — fax is the universal translator
- Misdirected faxes are the **leading cause of unauthorized disclosure of personal health information** in Ontario — **~5,000 privacy breaches** in 2021 alone
- **88% of hospital administrators say fax delays impact patient care**
- Canada Health Infoway launched a 3-year initiative (2024) to standardize eReferral/eConsult nationally

**Biggest leverage point:** An intermediary that ingests faxes AND digital referrals into a unified tracking system, so providers can adopt at their own pace.

Sources:
- CMA - Why Do Doctors Still Use Fax Machines
- CBC - Ontario to Pull the Plug on Fax Machines
- Canada Health Infoway - eReferral/eConsult

---

### 3. Care Transitions: Hospital to Home

- **Nearly 1 in 5 Medicare beneficiaries** had unplanned readmission within 30 days
- **75%+ of those readmissions were potentially preventable** — costing **~$12 billion/year**
- **50% of readmitted patients had zero contact with a physician** between discharge and readmission
- Physical function information included in discharge summaries only **26% of the time**
- Care Transitions Intervention: Reduced 30-day readmissions to **8.3% vs 11.9%** (control), saved **$3,762/patient**
- Warm handoffs do NOT significantly increase follow-up attendance (OR = 0.96, p = .71) — the concept is sound but execution is unreliable

Sources:
- Care Transitions - Making Healthcare Safer III (NCBI)
- Avoiding Preventable Readmissions - Commonwealth Fund

---

### 4. Information Not Following the Patient / Duplicate Tests

- **1 in 4 transferred patients undergo duplicate imaging** — most unnecessary
- **$1,100 added to patient care costs** per duplicate test/treatment delay
- **22% of hospitals do not integrate** received electronic health information
- **48% of hospitals report one-sided sharing** — share data but don't receive
- **14% of records contain incorrect patient data** due to interoperability failures
- **20% of safety hazard reports** from computer use in healthcare are related to information transfer

Sources:
- Effect of Shared EHRs on Duplicate Imaging - PMC
- Interoperable Exchange 2023 - ONC

---

### 5. Wait Times and Surgical/Imaging Backlogs (Canada)

- **28.6 weeks median wait** from GP referral to treatment in 2025 — **208% longer than 1993**
- **1.4 million Canadians** waiting for treatment in 2025
- Economic cost: **$3,043 lost per waiting patient** in wages; **$4.2 billion total** lost annually
- Longest waits: **neurosurgery (49.9 weeks)**, **orthopedic surgery (48.6 weeks)**
- Worst provinces: **New Brunswick (60.9 weeks)**, **PEI (49.7 weeks)**, **Nova Scotia (49.0 weeks)**
- MRI average wait: **84 days**; CT scan: **66 days**
- Economic cost of imaging waits: **$18 billion in lost patient income**, **$64 billion in lost GDP**

**Biggest leverage point:** Centralized wait list management with intelligent triage — routing to next available provider rather than a specific provider's queue.

Sources:
- Fraser Institute - Waiting Your Turn 2025
- CIHI - Wait Times for Priority Procedures 2025

---

### 6. Hospital Bed Management and ALC ("Bed Blockers")

- Canada has **7.7 acute beds per 1,000 people** vs OECD average of **14.7** — ranked **31st of 38**
- Many hospitals operate at **85%+ occupancy**
- ALC patients occupy valuable acute beds for weeks/months
- Hospital overtime hours: **<3% in 2019 to ~5% in 2023**

**Biggest leverage point:** Real-time discharge readiness tracking + automated community resource matching.

---

### 7. Appointment No-Shows

- National average no-show rate: **5-8%** (all specialties), **18-20%** for primary care
- Cost to US healthcare: **$150 billion/year**
- Self-scheduling tools reduce no-shows by **29%**
- AI-based appointment systems increased attendance by **10%/month**
- **67.3% of patients prefer text message reminders**

**Biggest leverage point:** Predictive no-show modeling + automated waitlist backfill.

---

### 8. Care Fragmentation and Coordination Gaps

- Physicians still do not consistently communicate about shared patients
- High fragmentation = increased ED visits, unnecessary testing, medication overuse, avoidable hospitalizations
- **$257 billion** in administrative complexity costs driven by fragmentation

**Biggest leverage point:** A shared patient timeline visible to all providers with automated alerts for gaps.

---

### 9. Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) Screening

- Common domains: housing, safety/violence, food/nutrition, income, transportation, social support
- Tools exist (PRAPARE, WellRx, HealthBegins) but screening without connecting to resources creates frustration
- Key gap: connecting screening results to actual community resources in a closed loop

---

### 10. Hospital Operations: 2025 C-Suite Priorities

- **84% of hospitals** adopting digital tools to reduce admin burden
- **83%** restructuring operating models
- **82%** establishing hire-to-retire workforce strategies
- Top concerns: workforce shortage, financial demands, tech risks, cybersecurity

---

### Operations Synthesis

The same structural problem appears repeatedly: **information and accountability do not flow between entities involved in a patient's care.** This manifests as:

1. Referrals sent into the void (no tracking, no closure)
2. Discharge summaries that arrive late or never
3. Tests repeated because results don't transfer
4. Wait lists managed per-provider rather than system-wide
5. SDOH needs identified but never connected to resources
6. No-show slots wasted because no automated backfill exists

**The common thread: the absence of a shared, real-time operational layer that tracks patients across organizational boundaries.**

## Vulnerable Populations

### 1. Caregivers of Elderly Parents

- 7.8 million Canadians provide unpaid care, averaging 30+ hours/week
- 89% have never received a formal support assessment
- 80% cannot access services outside regular hours
- Half experience financial stress; 22% spend $1,000+/month out-of-pocket
- 45% cite "difficulty navigating the healthcare system" as top barrier
- 94% say they need better access to home care services
- 74% of adult caregivers and 86% of sandwich generation caregivers report negative health effects

Sources:
- Canadian Centre for Caregiving Excellence - Caring in Canada
- Statistics Canada - Living with a Life-Limiting Illness 2024
- CIHI - Caregiver Distress

---

### 2. Immigrants and Newcomers

- Language is the #1 barrier. 60.7% use friends/family as interpreters
- Patients who speak neither official language experience **30% higher rate of harmful events** in hospital
- 3-month waiting period for provincial health insurance leaves newcomers uncovered
- System confusion: don't understand how insurance works, how to find a doctor, what is covered
- Result: misdiagnosis, delayed treatment, loss of trust, people stop seeking care

Sources:
- CIHI - Language Barriers and Hospital Harm
- Barriers and Solutions to Healthcare Access for Immigrants - Longwoods
- ICES - Language-Concordant Care

---

### 3. Indigenous Peoples

- 2024 scoping review of 66 studies: most racist experiences are covert — treated differently, ignored, stereotyped
- 1 in 5 Indigenous people report unfair treatment from a healthcare professional
- Joyce Echaquan died in 2020 after livestreaming racist abuse from staff. Joyce's Principle still not fully adopted
- Jurisdictional complexity: federal, provincial, and band-level gaps
- Geographic isolation: many communities fly-in only
- Historical trauma means the system itself is a site of harm

Sources:
- Anti-Indigenous Racism in Canadian Healthcare: Scoping Review 2024
- CBC - Joyce Echaquan Five Years Later
- CPHA - When Health Care Harms

---

### 4. People with Disabilities and Chronic Illness

- 71% cite availability of care providers/specialists as top barrier
- 45% report difficulty navigating the system
- 32% experienced physical barriers accessing facilities (Toronto data)
- 52-59% reported complications from delayed support
- 71% visited ERs in the past year — system failing to provide care before crisis
- Youngest group (15-44) hardest hit: 72% reported worsened conditions from lack of timely support

Sources:
- Statistics Canada - Living with Life-Limiting Illness 2024
- Healthcare System Barriers for Canadians with ME
- Canada.ca - Disability: A Chronic Omission in Health Equity

---

### 5. Low-Income Canadians and Medication Access

- 21% of Canadian adults have no prescription drug insurance
- 17% of uninsured report non-adherence due to cost (2.5x insured rate)
- Canada is one of only 4 OECD nations without publicly funded national medication insurance
- Pharmacare legislation (2024) covers only contraceptives and diabetes medications so far
- People skip doses, split pills, or don't fill prescriptions — leading to preventable hospitalizations

Sources:
- Statistics Canada - Inequities in Pharmaceutical Access
- Canada.ca - Pharmacare Legislation 2024

---

### 6. Mental Health Crisis

- 16.1 million unscheduled ED visits in 2024-2025
- ~500,000 Canadians left EDs without being seen in 2024
- Patients who leave face **14% higher risk of death or hospitalization within 7 days**
- People in crisis have the least capacity to advocate, wait 8+ hours, or navigate the system
- Montfort Hospital model (dedicated mental health emergency zone) showed **50% fewer incidents**

Sources:
- CIHI - Emergency Department Visits and Lengths of Stay
- ICES - LWBS Patients Face Higher Death Risk
- CBC - Montfort Hospital Mental Health Emergency Zone

---

### 7. Seniors and the Digital Divide

- 94% of Canadians aged 15-44 regularly use the internet vs 71% of those 75+
- Digital health literacy scores: 29 for ages 65-74, 21.7 for 75+
- Patient portals designed for digitally literate users
- "Digital first" during COVID actively excluded seniors
- Rigid approaches lead to "frustration, disengagement, and lack of confidence"
- **The portal IS the barrier**

Sources:
- JMIR - eHealth and the Digital Divide Among Older Canadians 2025
- JMIR Aging - Key Challenges to Digital Literacy for Older Adults 2026
- McMaster - Patient Portals for Older Adults

---

### 8. Rural and Remote Communities

- 20% of Canadians live rural, only 8% of physicians practice there
- Travel 35+ km to doctor, 30+ km to nearest hospital
- ER closures leave communities without timely access
- Virtual care requires internet many rural communities lack
- Solutions must work offline, over phone, or on minimal bandwidth

Sources:
- CIHI - Rural Health Care in Canada
- CMA - Does Where I Live Affect My Health Care
- PMC - Healthcare Experiences of Rural-Living Canadians 2025

---

### 9. People Experiencing Homelessness

- ~235,000+ Canadians experience homelessness in a given year
- Can't get a health card without an address — locked out of "universal" care
- Can't make appointments: no phone, no address, no callbacks
- Can't do follow-up: nowhere to recuperate, no one to act as caregiver
- Medical records scattered across providers with no coordination
- Hospitals building housing because discharging to street guarantees readmission

Sources:
- Homeless Hub - Health and Homelessness
- CMAJ - Homelessness is a Health Crisis
- PMC - Healthcare Costs of Homelessness in Toronto

### Cross-Cutting Themes for Design

| Theme | Who It Affects Most | Design Implication |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Health literacy gaps** | Newcomers, seniors, low-income, cognitive disabilities | Plain language. Visual aids. No jargon. Grade 6 reading level. |
| **Language barriers** | Newcomers, francophone minorities, Indigenous language speakers | Multilingual from day one. Professional interpretation, not Google Translate. |
| **Digital literacy barriers** | Seniors (75+), homeless, low-income, rural | Phone/voice must be first-class. Cannot be digital-only. Fewer steps, larger text. |
| **Transportation barriers** | Rural, elderly, disabled, low-income, homeless | Virtual options on low bandwidth. Asynchronous over synchronous. Location-aware. |
| **Cultural safety** | Indigenous, newcomers, racialized communities | Trust must be earned. Representation matters. Community health workers as intermediaries. |
| **System navigation complexity** | Caregivers, chronically ill, anyone without a family doctor | The system IS the barrier. Navigator role is highest-impact intervention. |
| **Caregiver coordination burden** | Family caregivers (sandwich generation, racialized) | Support caregiver AS a user, not just the patient. Shared access. |
| **Financial barriers** | Low-income, uninsured, homeless, newcomers | Transparent cost info. Connection to assistance programs. Never assume insurance. |

**Key insight:** The people most harmed by the Canadian healthcare system are the people with the least capacity to navigate it. Any solution that adds complexity, requires digital literacy, or assumes a stable life situation will widen the gap rather than close it.

## Role Perspectives

### 1. The Patient (unattached, no family doctor)
*"I don't know where to go, so I go to the ER."*

- 6.5 million Canadians have no family doctor. 25% have given up looking
- 500,000 left ERs without being seen in 2024
- Referrals vanish: 50% never completed, 65% never result in a scheduled appointment
- Test results fall into a void
- Single most repeated wish: "I just want someone that helps me figure out what to do next and makes sure nothing falls through the cracks"

### 2. The Clinician (drowning in admin)
*"I'm seeing more patients than I can safely manage."*

- For every 15 min with a patient, 9 min charting
- 85% do pajama time — 53 full days/year of after-hours EHR work
- 18.5 million hours/year wasted on unnecessary admin in Canada (= 55.6 million patient visits)
- 65% of Ontario family doctors plan to leave within 5 years
- Alert fatigue: 90-96% of clinical alerts overridden
- #1 thing they say would help: reduce admin burden

### 3. The Caregiver (unpaid, untrained, exhausted)
*"I became my mother's case manager and nobody told me how."*

- 7.8 million Canadians provide unpaid care, 30+ hours/week
- 89% never received a formal support assessment
- 80% can't access services outside regular hours
- 45% cite "difficulty navigating the system" as top barrier

### 4. The Newcomer / Immigrant
*"I don't understand how this system works and nobody explained it to me."*

- Language barriers cause 30% higher rate of harmful events in hospital
- 60.7% use friends/family as interpreters
- 3-month gap before provincial health insurance
- System assumes English literacy and cultural familiarity they don't have

### 5. The Senior (digital divide victim)
*"I can't use the portal. I can't see the text. I don't have the password."*

- Digital health literacy: 29 for ages 65-74, 21.7 for 75+
- Patient portals designed for digitally literate users
- Phone-based and in-person alternatives disappearing
- The portal IS the barrier

### 6. The Rural Patient
*"The nearest hospital is an hour away and the ER might be closed."*

- 20% of Canadians live rural, only 8% of physicians practice there
- Travel 35+ km to see a doctor
- Virtual care requires internet many lack
- Solutions must work offline, over phone, or on minimal bandwidth

### 7. The System Operator (admin, scheduler, coordinator)
*"Information doesn't flow. We're managing queues with spreadsheets and fax machines."*

- 152 million faxes/year in Ontario. 5,000 privacy breaches from misdirected faxes
- 1 in 4 transferred patients get duplicate imaging
- 7.7 acute beds per 1,000 vs OECD average of 14.7
- 18-20% no-show rate for primary care
- 28.6 weeks median wait — 208% longer than 1993

### 8. The Health Equity Advocate
*"The people most harmed have the least capacity to navigate."*

- Indigenous: 1 in 5 report unfair treatment from healthcare professionals
- Homeless: can't get health card without address — locked out of "universal" care
- 21% of adults have no prescription drug insurance
- Women and racialized patients disproportionately dismissed for pain
- Every system assumption fails for the most vulnerable

### 9. The AI/Technology Strategist
*"What's actually ready for adoption vs. what's technically cool?"*

- 176 AI initiatives in Canada. Only 12% scaled. Only 5% system-wide
- AI scribes: real but modest gains (41 seconds per note in UCLA study)
- 90-96% of existing alerts are overridden — more alerts will fail
- "Don't automate a broken process" — fix the process first
- What gains traction = alignment with real workflows
