## Synthetic Patient Personas

### MOCK PATIENT 1: Maria Santos — The Newcomer
- **ID:** SYN-PAT-001
- **Age:** 34
- **Background:** Arrived from Philippines 2 months ago. Speaks Tagalog and limited English. Husband works nights. Two children (ages 4 and 7).
- **Health situation:** Persistent abdominal pain for 3 weeks. No family doctor. No provincial health insurance yet (in 3-month waiting period). Went to walk-in clinic, told to go to ER. Went to ER, waited 6 hours, left without being seen because children were with her.
- **Barriers:** Language, no insurance yet, no family doctor, no childcare, doesn't understand the system, afraid of costs.
- **What she needs:** Someone to explain her options in plain language. Connection to a community health centre that serves uninsured newcomers. Follow-up to make sure the pain is investigated.
- **Research basis:** Newcomer barriers (30% higher harmful events from language barriers), 3-month coverage gap, system navigation confusion.

### MOCK PATIENT 2: Robert Chen — The Orphaned Patient
- **ID:** SYN-PAT-002
- **Age:** 58
- **Background:** Family doctor retired 14 months ago. On provincial waitlist for new GP. Has been to 3 different walk-in clinics in the past year.
- **Health situation:** Type 2 diabetes, hypertension, elevated cholesterol. Medications need regular monitoring. Last A1C was 7 months ago at a walk-in — results were sent to his old doctor's closed practice. Nobody followed up. Now experiencing numbness in feet.
- **Barriers:** No continuity of care. Each walk-in visit starts fresh. Test results lost. No one managing his chronic conditions holistically.
- **What he needs:** A care plan that persists across providers. Someone tracking that his A1C is overdue. A way to find a doctor or NP accepting patients.
- **Research basis:** 6.5M Canadians without family doctor, test results falling into void, "one issue per visit" at walk-ins.

### MOCK PATIENT 3: Dorothy "Dot" MacLeod — The Senior
- **ID:** SYN-PAT-003
- **Age:** 81
- **Background:** Lives alone in small-town Nova Scotia. Daughter lives 3 hours away in Halifax. Husband passed 2 years ago — he used to manage all appointments and medications.
- **Health situation:** Heart failure (managed), osteoarthritis, mild cognitive decline. Takes 7 medications. Was discharged from hospital 5 days ago after a fall. Discharge summary said "follow up with family doctor in 1 week." She forgot. Daughter didn't know she was discharged.
- **Barriers:** Digital divide (can't use patient portal), mild cognitive decline, lives alone, rural (nearest hospital 45 min), daughter not in the loop.
- **What she needs:** Phone-based check-in post-discharge. Daughter notified and authorized as caregiver. Medication reconciliation. Transportation to follow-up.
- **Research basis:** Digital divide (literacy score 21.7 for 75+), care transitions failure (50% no physician contact post-discharge), rural access, caregiver coordination gap.

### MOCK PATIENT 4: James Whiteduck — The Indigenous Patient
- **ID:** SYN-PAT-004
- **Age:** 42
- **Background:** Anishinaabe, lives on-reserve in Northern Ontario. Nearest hospital is 2-hour drive. Community health station has a nurse 3 days/week.
- **Health situation:** Referred to orthopedic specialist in Sudbury 8 months ago for knee injury that limits his ability to work. Referral was faxed. He has heard nothing. Called the specialist office twice — put on hold, then disconnected. Called his community health nurse — she doesn't have access to the hospital's system to check.
- **Barriers:** Geographic isolation, referral black hole, jurisdictional complexity (federal vs provincial healthcare), past negative experiences with healthcare system (dismissive, stereotyped), no way to track referral status.
- **What he needs:** Referral tracking that works across jurisdictions. Someone to advocate/follow up. Telehealth option for initial specialist consult. Cultural safety.
- **Research basis:** Referral black hole (50% never completed), Indigenous racism in healthcare (1 in 5 report unfair treatment), rural/remote access, fax machine problem.

### MOCK PATIENT 5: Amina Hassan — The Caregiver
- **ID:** SYN-PAT-005
- **Age:** 47
- **Background:** Single mother, works part-time as PSW. Caring for her mother (72, diabetes, early dementia) who lives with her. Also has a 15-year-old son.
- **Health situation (mother):** Mother sees 4 different providers. Amina manages all appointments, medications, and communication between them. Mother was prescribed a new medication by the specialist that interacts with one prescribed by the GP — Amina caught it herself by Googling.
- **Barriers:** No single view of mother's care. Providers don't communicate with each other. Amina is doing unpaid coordination on top of paid PSW work and parenting. No formal caregiver assessment. Exhausted.
- **What she needs:** A shared care plan visible to all providers AND to her as caregiver. Medication interaction alerts that actually work. Respite care information. Financial support navigation.
- **Research basis:** 7.8M unpaid caregivers, 89% never assessed, medication reconciliation failures, care fragmentation, caregiver burnout.

### MOCK PATIENT 6: Tyler Brooks — The Mental Health Crisis
- **ID:** SYN-PAT-006
- **Age:** 23
- **Background:** University student. No family doctor. Referred to psychiatrist 5 months ago by campus health — still waiting. Private therapy at $180/session is unaffordable.
- **Health situation:** Worsening anxiety and depression. Went to ER last week during a panic attack. Waited 9 hours. Was assessed, given a prescription, told to "follow up with your family doctor" (he doesn't have one). Prescription runs out in 2 weeks.
- **Barriers:** No family doctor, can't afford private care, public psychiatry wait is months, ER is not designed for mental health, prescription continuity gap.
- **What he needs:** A pathway to mental health care that doesn't require a family doctor. Community mental health resources. Prescription continuity. Crisis alternatives to ER.
- **Research basis:** Ontario psychiatry wait ~4 months, psychologists not covered, mental health triaged below physical in ER, 500,000 left ERs without being seen.

## Synthetic Provider Personas

### MOCK PROVIDER 1: Dr. Sarah Okonkwo — Family Physician
- **ID:** SYN-PRV-001
- **Role:** Family doctor, solo practice, urban Ontario
- **Panel:** 1,400 patients (was 1,100 two years ago — absorbed orphaned patients from retired colleague)
- **Reality:** Spends 3 hours/day on documentation after clinic hours. Sends referrals by fax — has no way to know if they were received. Gets 40+ patient portal messages daily. Considering dropping to part-time.
- **What she needs:** Documentation that completes during the visit. Referral tracking. Inbox triage. More time with patients, less time with screens.

### MOCK PROVIDER 2: Nurse Practitioner Alex Tremblay
- **ID:** SYN-PRV-002
- **Role:** NP at a community health centre, semi-rural Quebec
- **Panel:** 800 patients, growing weekly as new patients are assigned
- **Reality:** One admin clerk shared with two other NPs. Spends 40% of time on charting. Does everything a family doctor does but with less admin support. Patients with complex needs (newcomers, chronic illness, mental health) take longer but the system measures volume.
- **What they need:** Streamlined documentation. Better coordination with specialists. SDOH screening that actually connects to resources. Admin support.

### MOCK PROVIDER 3: Dr. Michael Park — Emergency Physician
- **ID:** SYN-PRV-003
- **Role:** ER physician, mid-size Ontario hospital
- **Reality:** Sees 30-40 patients per shift. At least 8-10 are there because they have no family doctor and nowhere else to go. Admits patients who then wait 12+ hours for a bed. Alert fatigue: overrides 90%+ of CDS alerts because they're irrelevant. Writes discharge instructions knowing patients won't follow up because they can't.
- **What he needs:** Fewer non-emergent patients in his ER. Discharge plans that actually connect to follow-up. Alerts that matter.

## Synthetic Referral Scenarios

### MOCK REFERRAL 1: The Vanished Referral
- **ID:** SYN-REF-001
- **From:** Dr. Okonkwo (SYN-PRV-001) → Orthopedic surgeon
- **For:** Robert Chen (SYN-PAT-002), knee pain limiting mobility
- **Sent:** Faxed 4 months ago
- **Status:** Unknown. Fax confirmation shows "delivered" but specialist office has no record. Robert thinks he's on the waitlist. Dr. Okonkwo assumes the specialist received it. Nobody has checked.
- **Outcome without intervention:** Robert waits another 6 months, condition worsens, ends up in ER.

### MOCK REFERRAL 2: The Rejected Referral Nobody Told About
- **ID:** SYN-REF-002
- **From:** Walk-in clinic doctor → Gastroenterologist
- **For:** Maria Santos (SYN-PAT-001), abdominal pain
- **Sent:** Faxed 6 weeks ago
- **Status:** Specialist rejected referral because required prerequisite tests (blood panel, ultrasound) were not included. Rejection faxed back to walk-in clinic. Walk-in clinic filed the fax. Nobody called Maria. Maria thinks she's waiting for an appointment.
- **Outcome without intervention:** Maria's condition worsens. She returns to ER. Cycle repeats.

### MOCK REFERRAL 3: The Successful Closed Loop
- **ID:** SYN-REF-003
- **From:** NP Tremblay (SYN-PRV-002) → Cardiologist
- **For:** Dot MacLeod (SYN-PAT-003), post-discharge cardiac follow-up
- **Sent:** Via eReferral platform
- **Status:** Received, acknowledged, appointment booked for 3 weeks out. Patient and daughter notified by text and phone call. NP can see appointment date in system. Reminder sent 48 hours before.
- **Outcome:** Dot attends appointment. Results sent back to NP same day. Care plan updated.
